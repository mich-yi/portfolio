overview

Project Context

01
The problem
Inconsistent, crowded, disconnected.

Inconsistent layouts, crowded interfaces, and disconnected user flows make navigation and shopping feel overwhelming. Core features like lists, browsing, and product discovery lack clarity and visual hierarchy.

02
The solution
Simplified, cohesive, intentional.

We redesigned the Costco mobile app to create a more intuitive and cohesive shopping experience through simplified navigation, and information architecture, clearer visual hierarchy, and improved visual consistency.

Shoppers feel stressed when long lines and crowding make their trip unpredictable and inefficient.

Users disengage from the app when it feels slow, cluttered, or difficult to complete basic tasks.

05

Users only see value in the Costco app when it provides clear membership-related benefits and easily visible deals and sales information.

reflection

What we learned.

Co-leading the project taught me that good collaboration isn't about dividing screens equally. We spent a significant amount of time aligning on research findings, design priorities, and interaction patterns before moving into high-fidelity design. That shared understanding made the final experience feel much more cohesive.

At the start of the project, many of the issues appeared visual. As our research progressed, it became clear that navigation, information architecture, and task flow had a much greater impact on the overall experience than changing colors or typography alone.

One of the biggest challenges was simplifying the experience without oversimplifying the product. Costco offers dozens of services across shopping, memberships, pharmacy, travel, and warehouse experiences. Rather than removing functionality, we focused on improving information architecture and prioritizing the features users needed most.

With more time, I'd
  • Prototype and test the redesigned navigation to validate whether it measurably improves task completion rates before moving into development.
  • Narrow the scope even further — focusing the redesign on the single highest-impact flow rather than touching every section at once.
