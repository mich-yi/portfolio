CartSplit.

Splitting groceries shouldn't require a two-hour roommate discussion. CartSplit helps roommates coordinate purchases, split costs, and manage payments in one collaborative experience.

01 — The Challenge

Five roommates. One surprisingly difficult conversation.

Before we ever bought our first shared grocery, my four roommates and I spent nearly two hours sitting in our living room trying to answer what seemed like simple questions.

Is this shared or personal?
If only 3 of 5 people want an item, who pays?
Can someone eat it if they didn't contribute?
How do we split one Costco receipt fairly?
What if people eat very different amounts?

Every answer created another question. If someone bought eggs, could everyone eat them, or only the people who'd contributed? What happened when someone wanted an expensive snack no one else did? By the end of the conversation, we'd settled on a system that technically worked, but it wasn't pretty.

Key insights
The problem wasn't unique to us, but it was more nuanced than we expected.
01

Every household already had its own informal system. Alternating payments, strict 50/50, majority-rules, or nothing at all, roommates had improvised a system long before any app could help.

02

Equal splitting wasn't always perceived as fair. When people consumed different amounts, even a simple 50/50 split quietly created guilt, resentment, or people opting out of splitting altogether.

03

Users wanted coordination, not inventory reminders. Manual restocking alerts had little value; what people actually needed was an easier way to coordinate what they were already buying together.

03 — From Insight to Solution

Item-based, not evenly split.

Research showed that roommates already had ways to organize groceries. The real friction came from coordinating shared purchases in a way that felt fair without awkward conversations or manual calculations. Splitting groceries wasn't really a budgeting problem. It was a coordination problem. Instead of asking roommates to split every bill evenly, CartSplit introduces an item-based cost-sharing system. Users simply claim the items they plan to share, and the app automatically calculates each person's contribution as the list changes.

Rather than replacing existing roommate habits, the design supports the flexible ways people already divide groceries while making those systems easier to manage.

04 — Testing & Iteration

Designing with feedback, not assumptions.

We conducted think-aloud usability testing with two college students living with roommates, observing how they navigated the prototype and where confusion occurred.

Think-aloud protocol
The First-Timer

Walked through the prototype cold, with no context beyond the app's purpose. The layout read as intuitive and the buttons felt well thought out, but the "+" button only created new lists, with no way yet to add an item once inside one.

She also asked for a way to split the final total by budget rather than by who claimed what, and wanted her profile to feel more like her own rather than a stock placeholder.

What worked
  • ✓ The interface felt intuitive, with clear navigation and familiar interactions
  • ✓ Both participants said they'd realistically use the app with their own roommates
Where confusion emerged
  • ✕ Item claiming was unclear and difficult to understand
  • ✕ Shared interactions between users lacked visibility
  • ✕ Screen flow was incomplete, causing confusion after certain actions
  • ✕ Users wanted more profile customization and a budget-based splitting option
Design iterations
  1. Made the "+" button context-aware, adding a list from home and adding an item inside one, instead of one ambiguous action
  2. Increased visibility of shared interactions and system feedback, so it's clear when someone else has claimed an item
  3. Refined navigation and screen flow between key steps, including where a user lands after adding an item

Result

Design 1 scored higher in clarity (4.71 vs. 4.29) and caused less confusion (1.29 vs. 2.0), but Design 2 scored higher in overall satisfaction (4.86 vs. 4.0) and likelihood of use (4.71 vs. 3.57). We moved forward with Design 2, as it better aligned with user behavior and preferences, prioritizing search-based input, reducing interface clutter, and supporting more direct interaction with shared cost features.

06 — Final Experience

A collaborative grocery experience built around flexibility.

01

Home

  • User is able to sort through their active and past lists
  • Lists display creation date, roommate collaboration information, and details on price and item count
  • Recent activity displays real-time updates of roommate activity on the app
02

Create list

  • User is prompted to enter a list name
  • Store can be selected from a dropdown featuring grocery stores
  • Intended shopping date for the grocery list can be chosen through a calendar interface
03

Grocery list

  • List immediately highlights a breakdown of each contributor's share of the total cost
  • Each item displays the current contributors to that item's price
  • User is able to delete, change the quantity of, claim, or view the specific product details of each item
  • Costs adjust according to changes in product quantity and who the item is split between
  • Product details contain a larger product image and more detailed description of each item
04

Checkout

  • Order summary displays the total number of items and list total price
  • User's individual share of the cost is highlighted
  • App provides a cost breakdown with each list participant's share of the total cost
  • User can request the amount owed from each roommate directly in the checkout interface
  • User is able to select a payment method and complete checkout directly within the app
05

Profile & notifications

  • Profile displays the user's name, username, and profile picture
  • User is able to edit their profile, changing their name, username, or profile picture
  • Notifications are grouped by all notifications and unread notifications, to highlight urgency
  • Notifications include payment requests, payment notifications, and edits to lists, including when items are added, claimed, and removed
07 — Design Decisions

Tradeoffs behind the key choices.

Why

Roommates rarely divide groceries evenly.

Tradeoff

Needs clear visibility into who claimed each item.

Why

Tradeoff

Had to stay informative without feeling overwhelming.

Automatic Cost Calculation
Why

Manual math created unnecessary friction.

Tradeoff

Users still needed to see how totals were calculated.

08 — Reflection

What I learned about coordination, not just groceries.

I started this project trying to solve a problem I was experiencing with my own roommates. Research confirmed that the problem extended far beyond our apartment, but it also challenged several of my assumptions about what people actually needed.

Features I initially thought would be valuable, like restocking reminders, mattered far less than reducing the friction of coordinating shared purchases. The strongest ideas came directly from observing real roommate behaviors rather than designing around assumptions.

This project reinforced that user research is not just about validating an idea. It is about refining that idea until it fits naturally into how people already live.
