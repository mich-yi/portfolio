Project Context

01
the problem
Habits rarely stick.

Many people struggle with staying consistent and get discouraged when attempting to keep accountability with daily habits. Although there are many habit-tracking apps on the market, most lack the flexibility and customization to keep a diverse audience engaged and motivated.

02
the solution
A habit tracker built on personalization.

We designed Habitat to help users achieve their goals sustainably by incorporating personalization and behavioral science principles, so the app adapts to them, not the other way around.

From Conversations to Insight

Before we could design anything, we needed to understand how people actually relate to habits, not how we assumed they did.

We sat down with 12 users, asking 21 questions across 6 themes we cared about most.

Habit Formation
How would you define a habit, and how is that different from a task?
How do you feel when you complete one? When you don't?
Barriers & Bad Habits
Tell me about a bad habit you've tried to break.
What's stopped you from breaking it?
Building New Habits
Tell me about a time you tried to start a new habit? Why that one?
What barriers did you run into?
Tracking Systems
What's your experience with habit-tracking apps or systems?
What's worked? What hasn't?
Community
Would you want to share your progress with others, like an accountability partner?
Aesthetics vs. Function
When you're using an app, what do you prioritize: how it looks, or how it works?

Those 12 conversations produced dozens of individual observations. We wrote each one down and affinity-mapped them by hand, clustering repeated ideas until patterns emerged.

Highly influenced by external motivators & peer pressure
Tracking feels too tedious for an already-busy schedule
Need to see the benefit up front to even start
Prefer function over aesthetics, mostly
Motivation, consistency & accountability all have to work together
Community keeps people honest, but not everyone wants it
Most find existing tracking apps overwhelming
Completing a habit = a real, felt sense of reward
Reminders help, nagging doesn't
Want distractions removed, not more added
Rigid schedules and complicated journals don't survive real life
Habits take time, rushing the process backfires

framing the problem

Dozens of How Might We's

Our five insights pointed to three recurring themes: motivation & reward, simplifying tracking, and user engagement. We wrote How Might We's against each before converging on the three that would guide the design.

...motivate users to start tracking habits?
...help users stay engaged after habits form?
...incorporate rewards to support motivation?
...make tracking feel less demanding?
...encourage retention after habits form?
...teach users to build habits without stress?
...help users form habits without overwhelm?
...emphasize community for support?
...encourage consistency over time?
...fight demotivation mid-streak?
motivation & reward

How might we help users stay motivated to complete their habits, even after the initial excitement fades?

simplifying tracking

How might we make habit tracking feel effortless, so it integrates into daily routines instead of adding to them?

user engagement

How might we build in community and accountability features without making the app feel overwhelming?

BRINGING IT ALL TOGETHER

Final Solution

The final product combines our research findings into one cohesive experience.

01Onboarding · Templates · Explore

Lower the barrier to building a habit.

Insight

New users abandon habit apps at setup, before they've felt any benefit, if the first steps ask for too much configuration.

Decision

Users land straight into adding a habit from a library of preset templates, customizable with icons and colors, rather than a blank form. Explore surfaces more habits by category once they're ready to add another.

  • Preset templates
    A library of ready-made habit templates removes the blank-page problem of setting up a first habit from scratch.
  • Icon & color customization
    Habits can be personalized visually right away, making the app feel like theirs from the first minute.
  • Flexible goal setting
    Frequency, time of day, and reminders are optional, not required, so setup never feels like a form.
02Home · Challenges · Streaks

Make consistency feel rewarding.

Insight

Users disengage when progress feels invisible. A checked box isn't much of a reward for showing up day after day.

Decision

Streaks grow a visual forest instead of just a number, habits can be grouped for stacking, and weekly challenges give users a reason to come back beyond their own individual habits.

  • Growing forest streaks
    Consistency grows a visual forest instead of just incrementing a number, giving streaks a sense of place.
  • Habit stacking
    Related habits can be grouped together, mirroring how people naturally chain routines.
  • Weekly challenges
    Time-boxed challenges give users a reason to return beyond their own individual habits.
03Timer · Intentions · Breaks

Support focus, not just tracking.

Insight

Checking off a habit doesn't help users actually do the work. Many habits need dedicated, distraction-free time, not just a log.

Decision

Every focus session starts by setting an intention, then runs on customizable focus, short-break, and long-break durations, with completed tasks tracked automatically as the session runs.

  • Session intentions
    Setting an intention before a session gives the timer a purpose beyond just counting minutes.
  • Customizable durations
    Focus, short-break, and long-break lengths adapt to how each user actually works.
  • Task tracking
    Completed tasks are tracked automatically as each session runs, no manual logging required.
04Profile · Partner · Community

Build accountability beyond yourself.

Insight

Internal motivation fades. Users kept habits longer when someone else was paying attention to their progress too.

Decision

An accountability-partner feature and a community feed surface what friends are up to, with reminders that nudge users to stay on track alongside a partner, not just alone.

  • Accountability partner
    Pairing with a partner turns motivation into something shared, not something users have to sustain alone.
  • Community activity
    A feed of what friends are up to keeps progress visible beyond just the user's own habits.
  • Partner reminders
    Reminders nudge users to stay on track alongside their partner, not just alone.
05Analytics

Reflect progress over time.

Insight

Day-to-day habit tracking hides the bigger picture. Users wanted to see how far they'd actually come, not just today's checklist.

Decision

Daily overviews roll up into weekly, monthly, and yearly views, with visual charts summarizing completed, missed, and skipped habits, and progress that's easy to share as a milestone.

  • Weekly/monthly/yearly views
    Daily overviews roll up into longer timeframes, so progress reads as a trend, not just a checklist.
  • Visual charts & shareable progress
    Charts summarize completed, missed, and skipped habits, and progress is easy to share as a milestone.
reflection

What Habitat Taught Me

My first end-to-end UX project, and the one that changed how I think about design.

01

Starting Point

Habitat was my first UX project after joining Design Innovation, and I was the only new member on a six-person team. I'd never conducted interviews, built a competitive analysis, organized an IA, or completed an end-to-end process.

02

Biggest Growth

Stepping into a team where everyone else already had design experience pushed me to learn quickly, planning research, synthesizing findings, wireframing, prototyping, so I could contribute rather than just observe.

03

What I'm Proud Of

I experimented with illustration for the first time to give Habitat a more approachable, motivating personality, and saw how visual storytelling strengthens a product, not just its interface.

04

Looking Back

We explored a broad set of ideas: templates, analytics, accountability, challenges, social features, focus sessions. Today, I'd narrow scope earlier and validate fewer concepts more deeply before building so wide.

Before this project, I saw UX primarily as creating interfaces. After working through research, synthesis, testing, and iteration, I realized the interface is only the final expression of a much larger process, a shift that made me want to pursue product design professionally.
